THE SECOND MOON LANDING

After the success of Apollo 11, NASA quickly prepared for another mission to the Moon. Apollo 12 aimed to prove that lunar landings could be precise, safe, and repeatable.

The mission begins

Apollo 12 launched on November 14, 1969. Just seconds after liftoff, the rocket was struck by lightning twice, causing temporary system failures. Despite this, the crew remained calm, and the mission continued successfully.

The crew

  • Charles "Pete" Conrad - Commander
  • Alan L. Bean - Lunar Module Pilot
  • Richard F. Gordon Jr. - Command Module Pilot

The landing

On November 19, 1969, Apollo 12 landed on the Moon with incredible accuracy, just a short distance from the Surveyor 3 spacecraft. This showed that astronauts could land exactly where they planned.

Exploration and discovery

The astronauts spent more time exploring the Moon compared to Apollo 11. They collected rock samples, conducted experiments, and even retrieved parts of Surveyor 3 to study how materials survived in space.

Why it matters

Apollo 12 proved that Moon missions were not just possible—they were becoming reliable. It helped NASA prepare for even more advanced missions and deeper space exploration in the future.
